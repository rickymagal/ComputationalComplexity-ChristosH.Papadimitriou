# Chapter 2 — Sections 2.3 & 2.4: Multiple Strings and Linear Speedup

Once you have accepted the single-tape Turing machine as a model of computation, the natural next question is: does augmenting it make a difference? What if the machine has two tapes? Ten? What if each tape has its own independent cursor? These are not idle questions -- they are the first exercises in the book's central methodology, which is to study how models of computation relate to one another.

Theorem 2.1 answers the multi-tape question definitively: any k-string Turing machine operating in time f(n) can be simulated by a single-string machine in time O(f(n)²). The quadratic slowdown comes from the need to sweep back and forth across the single tape to simulate the multiple independent cursors. The palindrome example is illuminating here -- the 2-string machine solves it in O(n) time by copying the input to a second tape and comparing both strings simultaneously, while the single-string machine requires O(n²) by Theorem 2.1's bound, and indeed a matching lower bound can be shown (Problems 2.8.4 and 2.8.5). So multiple strings genuinely help -- but only quadratically. The *qualitative* computational power is unchanged.

This is the first instance of a pattern that will repeat many times in the book: model extensions that seem powerful turn out to be equivalent to the base model up to polynomial factors. RAM programs, nondeterministic machines (with one crucial exception), parallel models, and probabilistic machines all fit this pattern to varying degrees. The intellectual payoff is that polynomial-time computability is an enormously *stable* concept -- it doesn't depend on which reasonable model you choose to formalize it in. This is essentially the quantitative content of Church's Thesis, which Papadimitriou discusses in Section 2.6. The original thesis, due independently to Church and Turing in 1936 (Church, A. "An unsolvable problem of elementary number theory," *American Journal of Mathematics*, 58, 1936; Turing, A.M. "On computable numbers, with an application to the Entscheidungsproblem," *Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society*, 42, 1936), asserts that any effectively computable function is Turing-computable. What Sections 2.3 through 2.6 are collectively arguing is the stronger, quantitative version: any *reasonable* model of computation agrees with Turing machines not just on *what* is computable but on *what is computable in polynomial time*.

Section 2.4, on linear speedup, is one of those results that initially looks like a curiosity but is actually doing serious philosophical work. Theorem 2.2 says that if a language is in TIME(f(n)), then for any ε > 0, it is also in TIME(εf(n) + n + 2). In plain terms: any constant multiplicative factor in a time bound can be made arbitrarily small. The proof encodes m symbols of the original machine's alphabet into a single "super-symbol," allowing the new machine to simulate m steps in a constant number of its own steps. It's a trick reminiscent of increasing a computer's word length -- you pay a one-time cost to compress the input, then reap the benefit for the rest of the computation.

The consequence is that the O notation in time bounds is not just a convenience -- it is *forced on us* by the physics of the situation. If constants can be arbitrarily reduced by a change of encoding, then a theory built on precise constant factors would be a theory built on sand. The correct level of abstraction is the rate of growth, and the linear speedup theorem gives us the formal justification for working at that level. This is why Papadimitriou feels entitled to write f(n) = O(n²) and call it "quadratic," without specifying whether the constant is 7 or 700 -- those constants disappear under linear speedup.

There is one important caveat, which the text handles carefully: linear speedup applies to *superlinear* time bounds. A machine needs at least n steps just to read its input, so any "decent" time bound already satisfies f(n) ≥ n, and the theorem says the constant in the leading term can be crushed to ε. For *space* bounds, an analogous theorem (Theorem 2.3) holds as well. The upshot is that both time and space complexity are measured correctly at the level of growth rates, and the book can proceed with that understanding built into its foundations.
