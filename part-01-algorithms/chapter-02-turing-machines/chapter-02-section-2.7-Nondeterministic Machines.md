# Chapter 2 — Section 2.7: Nondeterministic Machines

Everything in the chapter so far has been about *deterministic* computation -- machines that, given an input, follow a single, fixed sequence of steps. Section 2.7 introduces nondeterminism, and this is where Chapter 2 stops being preparatory and starts being genuinely dangerous. Nondeterminism is the concept that, more than any other, separates complexity theory from the rest of computer science.

A nondeterministic Turing machine (NTM) differs from a deterministic one in a single, seemingly small way: the transition function δ is replaced by a transition *relation*, meaning that from any configuration, the machine may have multiple possible next moves. We say the machine *accepts* its input if *any* sequence of choices leads to the accept state. Equivalently -- and this is the picture to keep in mind -- you can think of the machine as spawning an exponential tree of parallel computation paths, and accepting if at least one path reaches acceptance. Or, if you prefer the "lucky guess" metaphor: the machine nondeterministically *guesses* a certificate and then verifies it.

The key theorem of Section 2.7 is that nondeterminism can be simulated deterministically, but at an exponential cost. Specifically, if a nondeterministic machine accepts in time f(n), a deterministic machine can simulate it in time 2^O(f(n)) by systematically exploring all computation paths. This is the *single exception* to the general principle that model extensions cost only polynomial factors -- nondeterminism appears to require genuinely exponential simulation, and nobody has proven otherwise or improved upon it in fifty years.

This is the crux of the P vs. NP question, stated in terms of machine models rather than complexity classes: can nondeterminism be simulated deterministically with only a polynomial overhead? The class NP is precisely the class of languages decidable by nondeterministic polynomial-time Turing machines, and P is the class decidable by deterministic polynomial-time machines. P = NP if and only if determinism and nondeterminism are polynomially equivalent for polynomial-time computation.

What makes nondeterminism so powerful as a concept -- and so resistant to simulation -- is that it captures the asymmetry between *search* and *verification* that we glimpsed in Section 1.3 with TSP. Verifying that a given tour is short enough is easy: just add up the distances. Finding a short tour seems to require searching an exponential space of possibilities. A nondeterministic machine "solves" the search problem by guessing the answer and verifying -- but the deterministic machine has to actually search. Whether that search can always be done efficiently is what nobody knows.

It is worth noting that nondeterminism as a mathematical concept predates its use in complexity theory. It appears in automata theory (Rabin and Scott, 1959, showed that nondeterministic finite automata are equivalent to deterministic ones -- but that equivalence, critically, does not carry over to Turing machines with resource bounds) and in formal language theory. What complexity theory did was turn nondeterminism into a *measure of hardness* rather than merely an alternative computational style. The question of whether nondeterminism is "really" more powerful than determinism at polynomial time bounds is the question that has organized a large fraction of theoretical computer science for the last half-century.

The space analogue of this question -- does nondeterministic logarithmic space equal deterministic logarithmic space, i.e., does L = NL? -- is also open, though Immerman and Szelepcsényi proved independently in 1987 (Immerman, N. "Nondeterministic space is closed under complementation," *SIAM Journal on Computing*, 17(5), 1988; Szelepcsényi, R. "The method of forced enumeration for nondeterministic automata," *Acta Informatica*, 26(3), 1988) that NL is closed under complementation, which was a major surprise. Chapter 7 will cover this result. The point here is that Section 2.7 plants the seed for an entire research program -- one that runs through Chapters 7, 8, 9, 14, 16, 17, 19, and beyond.

Taken as a whole, Chapter 2 has done something remarkable. It began with a machine so primitive it seems almost insulting, and ended with a concept -- nondeterminism -- that encodes what may be the deepest open problem in mathematics. The Turing machine was not a detour. It was the only foundation solid enough to support the weight of what comes next.
