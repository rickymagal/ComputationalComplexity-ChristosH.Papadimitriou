# Chapter 1 — Section 1.2: Maximum Flow (and Matching)

MAX FLOW and MATCHING are introduced here as successful algorithmic stories, and they are genuinely beautiful ones. But their deeper purpose in the book's architecture is to introduce *reduction* as an intellectual tool -- and to do so in its most favorable light, before the concept is turned on its head.

The key moment in the section isn't the augmentation algorithm. It's the observation that MATCHING reduces to MAX FLOW. Papadimitriou constructs a network from a bipartite graph and shows that a perfect matching exists if and only if the maximum flow equals n. This means we don't need a new algorithm for MATCHING at all: we already have one, because we already solved MAX FLOW. The *structure* of one problem maps onto the *structure* of another, and the polynomial-time solution transfers across.

This is the friendly face of reduction, and you should appreciate it before you encounter the sinister one. In Chapters 8 and 9, reductions will be used to argue that problems are *hard*: if problem A reduces to problem B and A is known to be intractable, then B must be too. The entire theory of NP-completeness is built on running this logical arrow in the direction of hardness rather than easiness. So the student who internalizes reduction here, as a natural, elegant, almost obvious technique, is being set up to understand Cook's theorem as something surprising about the nature of problems rather than something purely technical that you would learn to pass a class.

There's a second thing being built, which is more subtle. MAX FLOW itself initially appears to run in time O(n³C), which looks polynomial but secretly isn't, because C can be exponentially large in the input size. Papadimitriou fixes this by switching to shortest-path augmentation, recovering genuine polynomial-time performance. This moment is the first concrete demonstration of a lesson that recurs throughout the book: *what we count matters enormously*. The choice of input representation -- binary encoding of numbers rather than unary -- is not a neutral decision. It determines whether an algorithm is genuinely efficient or merely disguised as such. This concern about representation feeds directly into Chapter 2's careful discussion of Turing machines and how inputs are encoded, and it resurfaces in Chapter 10 when pseudo-polynomial algorithms for number problems are examined.

Finally, the MAX FLOW section introduces the reader to the experience of *iterative algorithmic improvement*, in other words, starting with a correct but inefficient algorithm and refining it through structural insight. This pattern (naive algorithm → identify bottleneck → fix with structural observation → repeat) is the experience of doing algorithms research, and Chapter 1 wants you to feel that rhythm before the book shifts its attention from "how do we solve this?" to "can it even be solved efficiently at all?" or even "can it be solved at all?"
